Sound is not background.
It's hospitality.

Mark Collins  ·  Lovely Anytime

There is a problem spreading through bars and restaurants in France, and it has nothing to do with the food or the service. It is the playlist. Specifically, the cover playlist — that endless rotation of familiar songs stripped of their original energy, repackaged into something smooth, inoffensive, and completely forgettable.

Walk into almost any wine bar, brasserie, or hotel lobby in Provence right now and you will hear it. A breathy acoustic version of something that was once urgent. A lounge remix of a song that used to mean something. Background music designed not to disturb — and in achieving that goal, it disturbs everything.

The room feels the same at noon as it does at midnight. That is not ambiance. That is wallpaper.

Creating the right environment is not only touch and feel and smell and taste. It is also sound. And sound is not a playlist you set once and forget. It is a living, moving element of the guest experience — one that should evolve through the day the way light does, the way the crowd does, the way the energy in a room shifts from the quiet of an early lunch service to the heat of a full evening.

A good morning service calls for something unhurried. Space between notes. Music that doesn't ask anything of the guest. By mid-afternoon, as the room fills and conversation picks up, the tempo can rise — subtly, naturally, the way a good sommelier reads a table. By evening, especially on a Friday, the music should carry the room forward. It should feel intentional. Curated. Like someone in the building actually cares.

The cover problem

The reason cover playlists have taken hold is convenience. Licensing original music is complicated and sometimes costly. Cover libraries sidestep that. But the cost paid elsewhere — in atmosphere, in distinctiveness, in the feeling a guest takes home with them — is far higher than any licensing fee.

Guests don't always know why they feel a certain way in a space. They can't always name it. But they feel it. They feel the difference between a room that has been thought about and one that hasn't. Music is one of the most immediate signals a space sends — and right now, too many spaces in France are sending the wrong one.

What to do instead

Build your own library. It takes time, but it is not complicated. Start with the feeling you want at each moment of the day and work backwards. Morning: what does calm, unhurried, and slightly warm sound like to you? Evening on a busy Saturday: what carries energy without aggression? The answers are personal — and that is the point. A playlist that reflects the identity of a place is one of the cheapest and most powerful branding tools available.

Vary the tempo deliberately. Not randomly — deliberately. Think of the arc of a day in your space and build music that moves with it. Assign someone ownership of it. Not as a task, but as a craft.

And please. Retire the covers.
